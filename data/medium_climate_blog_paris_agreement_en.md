# Ten Years After Paris: Are We Keeping Our Climate Promises?

A decade ago, representatives from 196 nations gathered in Le Bourget, France, to sign what would become the most ambitious international climate agreement in history. The Paris Agreement, formally adopted on December 12, 2015, set a landmark goal: limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels and no more than 2°C under any circumstances.

Ten years later, the picture is sobering.

## The Numbers Don't Lie

According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Sixth Assessment Report, global average temperatures in 2024 were 1.45°C above the 1850–1900 baseline — dangerously close to the threshold. Dr. Friederike Otto, a climate scientist at the Grantham Institute at Imperial College London, warned that the window for corrective action is "narrowing by the month."

Global CO₂ emissions reached a record 37.4 gigatons in 2023, according to the Global Carbon Project led by Professor Pierre Friedlingstein at the University of Exeter. Despite the rapid expansion of renewable energy in countries like Germany, China, and the United States, fossil fuel consumption remains near historically high levels. The International Energy Agency (IEA), based in Paris, noted that coal use in South and Southeast Asia partially offset gains made elsewhere.

## Who Is Falling Short?

The European Union, under its European Green Deal — championed by former European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen — has made measurable progress, cutting emissions by 32% since 1990. But Brazil, under President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva, faces ongoing international criticism for deforestation rates in the Amazon Basin, which reached 11,568 km² in 2024 according to Brazil's National Institute for Space Research (INPE) in São José dos Campos.

India, now the world's third-largest emitter after China and the United States, has pledged to achieve net zero by 2070 — a timeline many climate advocates, including Greta Thunberg and the Youth Climate Coalition, consider far too late to avert catastrophic warming. Australian advocacy group Climate Council has called the Indian timeline "a betrayal of Pacific Island nations."

## The Role of Fossil Fuel Lobbying

A landmark report published in September 2025 by InfluenceMap, a London-based think tank, found that major oil and gas companies — including Saudi Aramco, Shell, BP, and ExxonMobil — collectively spent $750 million on lobbying against climate legislation between 2020 and 2024. Shell's CEO Wael Sawan rejected the findings as "selective and misleading."

## A Fragile Hope

The upcoming COP30 summit in Belém, Brazil, scheduled for November 2025, will be a critical test of international resolve. UN Secretary-General António Guterres has urged all nations to submit updated Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) with more aggressive 2035 targets.

"We are not on track," said Dr. Katharine Hayhoe, chief scientist at The Nature Conservancy and professor at Texas Tech University. "But the tools to get there exist. What's missing is political will."

The renewable energy sector offers some genuine optimism. In 2024, solar power capacity globally surpassed 2 terawatts for the first time, with China alone adding 430 gigawatts. Danish wind energy company Ørsted and Spain's Iberdrola both reported record investment years. Meanwhile, battery storage costs have fallen 89% since 2010, according to BloombergNEF.

The next decade will determine whether the Paris Agreement becomes a turning point in human history — or a monument to broken promises.
