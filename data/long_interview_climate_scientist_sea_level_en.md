# Interview: Dr. Maria van den Berg on Sea Level Rise and the Future of the Dutch Delta

*Dr. Maria van den Berg is a professor of coastal hydrology at Delft University of Technology (TU Delft) and a senior fellow at Deltares, the Dutch research institute specialising in water, subsurface, and infrastructure. She leads the NWO-funded RISES-AM research programme and was a lead author on the IPCC Sixth Assessment Report's chapter on sea and coast. NRC journalist Tobias Schout spoke to her in her office in Delft.*

---

**The IPCC now projects between 0.3 and 1 metre of sea level rise by 2100 under moderate emissions scenarios. Is the Netherlands prepared?**

We are better prepared than almost any other country on earth. The Delta Works — the Oosterscheldekering, the Maeslantkering near Rotterdam, the Haringvlietdam — are engineering marvels that protect much of the southwestern Netherlands. The Maeslantkering alone, completed in 1997, is one of the largest moving structures ever built. But I would be lying if I said we are fully prepared for what the science is now telling us.

The honest answer is: we have prepared for the 20th century, and we are now facing the 21st. The current safety standards for primary flood defences, updated in 2017 under the Dutch Water Act, are based on scenarios that assumed 0.35 metres of rise by 2100. The IPCC's updated projections, particularly for a high-emission scenario (SSP5-8.5), now suggest we could see 0.8 to 1.0 metres by 2100 — and that is before we account for potential marine ice sheet instability in West Antarctica, which could add another 0.5 metres.

**You have used the term "deep uncertainty" in your recent publications. What does that mean for policymakers?**

Deep uncertainty is a technical term, but the concept is intuitive. We know sea levels will rise. We are confident of that. What we cannot be precise about is the rate, because that depends on human choices we haven't yet made — whether we keep warming at 1.5°C or allow it to reach 3°C or beyond — and on physical processes we don't yet fully understand, particularly ice dynamics in Greenland and Antarctica.

For a policymaker, that is deeply uncomfortable. You are investing billions of euros in infrastructure that needs to last a century, but you don't know exactly what conditions it will face. My recommendation, and the recommendation of the Deltacommissaris — currently Peter Glas — is to design adaptive pathways rather than fixed solutions. Build in flexibility. Plan for the worse end of the range.

**Is Rotterdam doing that?**

Rotterdam is one of the world's leaders in climate adaptation, and I say that without reservation. The Water Square at Benthemplein, designed by De Urbanisten, is a globally cited example of multifunctional urban design — a public square that becomes a water retention basin during heavy rain. The Rijnhaven floating residential district, the green roofs programme covering over 220,000 square metres, the Rotterdam Climate Adaptation Plan developed with the Municipality of Rotterdam — these are all serious, well-funded efforts.

But Rotterdam also has around 40 kilometres of port infrastructure that sits at or near the current high-water mark. If sea levels rise faster than projected, or if we get more compound events — storm surge combined with high river discharge — the port could face closures that cost hundreds of millions per day. The Port of Rotterdam Authority and the Ministry of Infrastructure and Water Management are aware of this. I sit on an advisory board to that ministry. The conversations are frank.

**You have been critical of the Dutch government's recent decision to cut funding for KNMI, the Royal Netherlands Meteorological Institute. Why?**

The KNMI has been producing world-class climate monitoring and modelling since 1854. It feeds data into the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) in Reading and into global models maintained by NOAA in the United States. The decision by the Schoof cabinet to cut KNMI's operational budget by €14 million — as part of broader austerity measures — is, in my view, a false economy.

We are spending tens of billions on flood defences. We need the best possible science to know how to design them. Cutting the scientific institution that generates that science is like building an expensive car and then removing the steering wheel. I wrote an open letter to the Minister of Infrastructure, Mona Keijzer, along with 47 other researchers from TU Delft, WUR, Utrecht University, and VU Amsterdam. We have not yet received a substantive reply.

**What gives you hope?**

Three things. First, young Dutch engineers. I supervise thirty PhD students and postdocs, many of them working on nature-based coastal solutions — living dikes, mangrove restoration, sand nourishment schemes. The intellectual quality is extraordinary. Second, the international momentum around the loss and damage fund agreed at COP27 in Sharm el-Sheikh, which will for the first time provide dedicated finance to countries facing irreversible climate impacts. Third, simply the fact that the Netherlands exists. We have been fighting water for a thousand years. We drained the Haarlemmermeer, we built the Zuiderzeewerken, we survived the 1953 North Sea flood and turned it into the Delta Works. We are not incapable of bold, long-term collective action. We just need to choose it again.
