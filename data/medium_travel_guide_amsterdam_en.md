# Amsterdam Travel Guide: What to See, Eat and Do in 2025

Amsterdam, the capital of the Netherlands, is one of Europe's most visited cities — yet it retains an intimate, village-like character that sets it apart from capitals like Paris or London. Home to 900,000 residents, it packs a remarkable density of world-class museums, historic architecture, diverse cuisine, and vibrant nightlife into a city you can largely explore on foot or by bicycle.

## Getting There and Around

Amsterdam Airport Schiphol, operated by the Royal Schiphol Group, is one of the busiest airports in Europe, with direct connections to over 300 destinations. From Schiphol, the Intercity Direct train reaches Amsterdam Centraal Station in 17 minutes (fare: €5.50 with OV-chipkaart, or €18.80 without).

Within the city, the GVB operates an extensive tram, metro, and bus network. Day passes cost €9.50. Cycling, however, is the true Amsterdam experience: rental companies like MacBike (€14.75/day) and Black Bikes (€12.50/day) operate near Centraal Station. Be aware that Amsterdam has 881,000 bicycles — more bikes than people.

## Must-See Museums

**Rijksmuseum** (Museumstraat 1) is the Netherlands' national museum and houses Rembrandt's *The Night Watch* along with 8,000 other works spanning 800 years of Dutch and Flemish art. Book tickets in advance (€22.50 adult); queues without reservations can exceed two hours.

**Van Gogh Museum** (Museumplein 6) holds the world's largest collection of works by Vincent van Gogh, including *The Potato Eaters* and *Almond Blossom*. Entry: €22 adult.

**Anne Frank House** (Westermarkt 20, Jordaan district) is the preserved hiding place where Anne Frank and her family hid from the Nazis between 1942 and 1944. Tickets must be booked weeks in advance online (€16 adult); same-day tickets are not available.

## Neighbourhoods

The **Jordaan** district, west of the main canal ring, is Amsterdam's most charming neighbourhood — a maze of narrow streets, independent galleries, vintage shops, and brown cafes (bruine kroegen). De Negen Straatjes (the Nine Streets) is a grid of boutique shopping streets beloved by locals and tourists alike.

For a grittier, more local experience, head to Amsterdam-Noord across the IJ River, accessible by the free NDSM ferry from behind Centraal Station. The NDSM Wharf, a former shipyard, now hosts creative studios, the Pllek beach bar, and frequent cultural events.

## Where to Eat

**Café de Jaren** (Nieuwe Doelenstraat 20) is a grand café with a sunny terrace overlooking the Amstel River — perfect for weekend brunch. For Indonesian rijsttafel — a Dutch-Indonesian tradition born from the colonial era — **Restaurant Blauw** on Amstelveenseweg is consistently rated among the best in the city. Budget-conscious travellers should seek out the traditional Dutch herring (haring) stands, where a broodje haring costs around €4.

## Practical Notes

Amsterdam enforces a tourist tax (toeristenbelasting) of €3 per person per night. The city has also banned new tourist shops in the historic centre and restricted Airbnb rentals to 30 nights per year since 2023, under mayor Femke Halsema's overtourism policies. Visitors are advised to be respectful in residential areas, particularly in the Red Light District (De Wallen), where photography of sex workers is strictly prohibited.
