# The EU AI Act Is a Genuine Achievement — but It Won't Save Us from Ourselves

When the European Parliament voted on March 13, 2024, to adopt the EU Artificial Intelligence Act — with 523 votes in favour, 46 against, and 49 abstentions — it completed a legislative journey that began in April 2021 under European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen. The vote marked the end of three years of often bruising negotiations between the Commission, the Council, and the Parliament, and it placed the European Union at the forefront of AI governance globally.

The Act is a genuine achievement. But in the year since it entered into force, the gaps between its ambitions and its implementation have grown alarmingly visible.

## What the AI Act Actually Does

The AI Act introduces a tiered risk framework. Systems classified as "unacceptable risk" — including social scoring systems of the kind deployed by authorities in China, and real-time biometric identification in public spaces — are banned outright. "High-risk" applications in areas like employment, credit scoring, education, and critical infrastructure must undergo conformity assessments and comply with transparency, data governance, and human oversight requirements.

"General-purpose AI" (GPAI) models — which includes large language models like OpenAI's GPT-4 and GPT-5, Google's Gemini, Meta's Llama, and Anthropic's Claude — face disclosure requirements and must publish technical documentation under Article 53. Those with systemic risk (defined as models trained on more than 10²⁵ floating-point operations) face additional obligations including model evaluations, incident reporting to the European AI Office in Brussels, and adversarial testing.

On paper, this is a serious regulatory architecture. The European AI Office, formally established within the Directorate-General for Communications Networks, Content and Technology (DG CONNECT), has a budget of €110 million and a staff of 140 as of early 2025.

## Where the Cracks Are Showing

The first problem is enforcement. The AI Act delegates most enforcement responsibility to national competent authorities in each EU member state. In practice, several smaller member states — including Bulgaria, Romania, and Cyprus — have yet to designate a competent authority more than a year after the Act's adoption. The Netherlands has appointed the Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens (AP) as its provisional AI supervisor, a decision that civil society organisations like Bits of Freedom and AlgorithmWatch have welcomed, though they warn the AP is underfunded for its expanded mandate.

The second problem is lobbying-induced dilution. Internal European Parliament documents leaked to The Guardian in February 2025 showed that lobbyists from Microsoft, Palantir, and IBM met with members of the Internal Market and Consumer Protection Committee (IMCO) more than 240 times during negotiations. The final text weakened provisions on remote biometric identification and narrowed the definition of "high-risk" systems in ways that largely benefit incumbents with large compliance departments.

The third problem is timing. The most consequential provisions — those governing GPAI models — don't apply until August 2025. By that date, OpenAI will likely have released GPT-5 and possibly GPT-6. The regulatory framework is, structurally, always a generation behind the technology it seeks to govern. This is not a new observation: Margrethe Vestager, the former Executive Vice President for A Europe Fit for the Digital Age, made exactly this point in her farewell speech to the Commission in November 2024.

## The GDPR Comparison

Proponents of the AI Act frequently invoke the GDPR as a model of effective European digital regulation. The General Data Protection Regulation, which entered into force in May 2018, genuinely changed global corporate behaviour: Apple, Google, and Meta all restructured data handling practices to achieve GDPR compliance, and California's CPRA was directly inspired by the European framework.

But the GDPR also took years to bite. It wasn't until January 2023 that the Irish Data Protection Commission (DPC) fined Meta €390 million for its legal basis for processing personal data in targeted advertising — five years after the Regulation entered into force. The Irish DPC, which supervises most major US tech platforms because they are headquartered in Dublin for EU purposes, has faced persistent criticism from Max Schrems and his advocacy group noyb (None of Your Business) for foot-dragging.

The AI Act will likely follow a similar trajectory. The early years will see landmark guidance documents and a few high-profile investigations. Meaningful fines — which the Act caps at €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover for prohibited AI practices — are unlikely before 2027 or 2028.

## What Would Actually Help

The AI Act will work better if accompanied by four things that are currently missing. First, a European sovereign compute infrastructure: the ability for European companies and researchers to train and run competitive AI models without depending on AWS, Microsoft Azure, or Google Cloud. The proposed €10 billion EuroHPC expansion is a start, but it is dwarfed by the $100 billion that Microsoft alone has announced for AI infrastructure investment in 2025.

Second, a genuine transatlantic AI governance dialogue. The Biden administration's AI Executive Order of October 2023 and the subsequent UK AI Safety Institute, founded by Matt Clifford and chaired by Yoshua Bengio, represent serious efforts. But they remain fragmented. A binding international agreement — even a narrow one covering only frontier AI systems — would be far more impactful than parallel national frameworks.

Third, civil society resourcing. AlgorithmWatch, Bits of Freedom, the Gesellschaft für Freiheitsrechte in Berlin, and Access Now do genuinely important work exposing AI harms. They operate on shoestring budgets compared to the compliance teams of the companies they monitor.

Fourth, and most fundamentally: a clearer answer to what we are actually trying to protect. The AI Act is framed around risk. But risk compared to what baseline? Compared to a world without AI, or compared to a world with unregulated AI? Those lead to very different regulatory philosophies. Europe has the right instinct — that technology must serve human dignity, as enshrined in the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights — but it has not yet translated that instinct into a coherent and enforceable standard.

The EU AI Act is better than nothing. It is better than what the United States has done. But it is not enough, and we should not mistake enacting it for having solved the problem.
